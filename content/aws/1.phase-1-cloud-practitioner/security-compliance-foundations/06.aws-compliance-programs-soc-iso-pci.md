# AWS Compliance Programs (SOC, ISO, PCI)

When your business handles sensitive data -- credit cards, health records, government information -- regulators and customers need **proof** that your infrastructure is secure. **Compliance programs** provide that proof through independent audits and certifications.

AWS holds dozens of compliance certifications. Your job is to know which ones matter for your use case and understand that **AWS compliance covers their side of the Shared Responsibility Model, not yours**.

Think of compliance like a building inspection. The landlord (AWS) gets the building certified for fire safety and structural integrity. But you (the tenant) are still responsible for not leaving the stove on or blocking fire exits.

### SOC Reports (Service Organization Controls)

SOC reports come from **independent auditors** who evaluate AWS's internal controls.

| Type | What It Covers | Who Needs It |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| **SOC 1** | Controls relevant to **financial reporting** | Companies whose cloud use affects financial statements |
| **SOC 2** | Controls for **security, availability, confidentiality, processing integrity, and privacy** | Most businesses -- the gold standard for cloud security audits |
| **SOC 3** | Same scope as SOC 2 but a **public summary** | Marketing and public trust -- anyone can read it |

**SOC 2** is the most commonly requested. When a customer asks "prove your cloud is secure," a SOC 2 report is usually the answer.

### ISO 27001

An international standard for **information security management systems (ISMS)**. It defines how an organization should manage risks to information security. AWS is ISO 27001 certified, meaning an independent auditor verified that AWS follows a rigorous security management framework.

**When it matters:** International organizations, European companies, and any business that needs globally recognized security certification.

### PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)

Required for any organization that **stores, processes, or transmits credit card data**. PCI DSS has 12 requirements covering network security, data encryption, access controls, and monitoring.

**AWS is PCI DSS Level 1 compliant** (the highest level). This means the AWS infrastructure that processes card data meets PCI standards. But your application and configurations must **also** be PCI compliant -- AWS cannot do that part for you.

| PCI Responsibility | AWS | Customer |
|-------------------|-----|----------|
| Physical security of servers | Yes | No |
| Network infrastructure encryption | Yes | No |
| Application security | No | Yes |
| Encryption of stored card data | No | Yes |
| Access control to cardholder data | No | Yes |

### HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)

A US law that protects **patient health information (PHI)**. AWS does not "certify" for HIPAA, but it offers **HIPAA-eligible services** and will sign a **Business Associate Agreement (BAA)** with healthcare organizations.

**Key requirements:**

- Use only HIPAA-eligible AWS services (S3, RDS, Lambda, and many others)
- Sign a BAA with AWS through the AWS Artifact portal
- Encrypt all PHI at rest and in transit
- Implement access controls and audit logging

### FedRAMP (Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program)

A US government program that standardizes security assessments for cloud services. AWS has **FedRAMP Authorization** at multiple levels, allowing federal agencies to use AWS for government workloads.

**When it matters:** US federal government agencies and contractors handling government data.

## Compliance and the Shared Responsibility Model

This is critical for the exam: **AWS compliance does not make you compliant automatically.**

| What AWS Certifies | What You Must Do |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| Physical infrastructure security | Secure your applications and data |
| Network and hypervisor controls | Configure encryption and access controls |
| Operational processes and procedures | Follow compliance requirements in your configurations |
| Availability of the platform | Architect your application for compliance |

AWS gives you the **tools** to be compliant (encryption services, logging, access controls). Using those tools correctly is **your responsibility**.

## Key Takeaways

- Compliance programs provide **independent proof** that security controls are in place
- **SOC 2** is the most commonly requested -- it covers security, availability, and confidentiality
- **PCI DSS** applies to credit card data, **HIPAA** to health records, **FedRAMP** to US government
- **ISO 27001** is the global standard for information security management
- AWS compliance covers **their** side of the Shared Responsibility Model -- you must still secure your data, configurations, and applications
- Compliance reports are available for download through **AWS Artifact**
